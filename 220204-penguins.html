What makes a manly penguin?

In humans, what makes a man visibly different from the opposite sex also makes him more appealing. Features like the broad shoulders, square jaws, and towering height form the picture of (stereotypical) manliness. I wanted to see if there are equivalents for penguins ‚Äî telltale signs of masculinity in these round, cushy waddling birds.

(I also just wanted an excuse to start this blog. The Palmer penguin data(1) is one of those ABC intro materials you're very likely to come across, if you're trying to learn anything data related. I thought this would be an interesting way to rediscover such a familiar dataset.)

The below visualization shows body measurements of 333(2) brush-tailed (also called rump-legged, or Pygoscelis) penguins, including the Ad√©lie, Chinstrap, and Gentoo species ‚Äî the 3 most commonly seen species accross the Antarctic Peninsula.

Weight shows the largest gender gap for all three species. This makes sense: a study of one Ad√©lie colony found that 17.3% of chick losses were from parent deserting the nest due to starvation, and since the returning mates arrived only a few days later, the loss could have been avoided if the parent could hold out for a little longer. In the waiting game, it's advantageous to be a large, fat penguin.(3) For mommy penguins, fatter husbands mean higher chance of survival for her babies.

On a few features, we can almost draw a line to separate male from female observations. On others, blue and green dots intermigle, with both genders occupying roughly the same range of measurements. This also makes sense: in humans, broad shoulders or sharp face shapes are not exclusive to men. Not one but several features together are better predictor of sex.

Side note: just from these graphs alone, it looks to me like plump, curvaceous women with significant lips would translate to very macho penguins, making them highly desirable to both species.

Limitations
The highlighted averages in these graphs don't reflect conclusions from the research paper that comes with the dataset. The paper suggests the strongest predictors of male are:

  • Weight and beak length in Ad√©lie
  • Weight and beak depth in Gentoo
  • Beak length and depth in Chinstrap

...with weight the least predictive feature for Chinstrap, and wing length a weak predictor for all three species. Comparing averages may be a weak way to determine predictive features, as it doesn't take into account the many overlaps in the observation ranges of two genders. A large overlap means we cannot draw a line to separate one gender's observations from the other's. Features with large overlaps, therefore, cannot be used to easily distinguish categories. For this classification problem, multivariate logistic regression and more advanced statistical analyses, like those performed in the research paper, would be a better way to go.
