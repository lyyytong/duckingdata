What makes a manly penguin?

In humans, what makes a man noticeably different from the opposite sex seems to make him more appealing. Physical features like the broad shoulders, square jaws, and superior height appear often as qualifiers of (stereotypical) manliness.

I wanted to see if there are equivalents for penguins ‚Äî visible features whose size signals strong masculinity. The below visualization was made from a dataset(1) of body measurements from 333(2) brush-tailed (also called rump-legged, or Pygoscelis) penguins, including the Ad√©lie, Chinstrap, and Gentoo species.

Weight shows the widest gender gap for all three species. This makes sense: a study of one Ad√©lie colony found that 17.3% of chick losses were from parent deserting the nest due to starvation, and since the returning mates arrived only a few days later, the loss could have been avoided if the parent could hold out for a little longer. In the waiting game, it's advantageous to be a large, fat penguin.(3) For mommy penguins, fatter husbands mean higher chance of survival for her babies.

While males are generally larger than females, the distinction can be subtle. On few features ‚Äî weight for Ad√©lie and Gentoo, beak depth for Gentoo, and to lesser extent beak length for Chinstrap ‚Äî we can almost draw a line to roughly separate observations of each gender. On others, blue and green dots migle over large areas, with both genders occupying the same range of measurements.

This also makes sense: in humans, broad shoulders or angular face shapes are not exclusive to men. Not one but a combination of several features may be more accurate predictor of sex.

This may be an overgeneralization, but it looks like plump, curvaceous women with significant lips would translate to very macho penguins, making them highly desirable to both species.

Limitations
The highlighted averages in this graph don't reflect conclusions from the research paper that accompanies the dataset. It suggests the strongest predictive features of male are:

  • Weight and beak length for Ad√©lie
  • Weight and beak depth for Gentoo
  • Beak length and depth for Chinstrap

With weight the least predictive feature for Chinstrap, and wing length a weak predictor for all three species. Beside showing the latter (wing length), the graph fails to reflect most of the rest. This may be because comparing averages is a weak way to determine predictive features: it doesn't take into account the many overlaps in the observation ranges of two genders. A large overlap means the feature cannot be used to easily distinguish categories. For this classification problem, multivariate logistic regression and more advanced statistical analyses, like those performed in the research paper, would be a better way to go.
