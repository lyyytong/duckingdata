263 years of spider discoveries

The World Spider Catalog(1) chronicles all spider species discovered between 1757 and present day — up to 2019 in this particular dataset. It lists a total of 47,714 recognized species, which such rich information on year and location of discovery that I think I got hiccups from excitement.

For ease of visualization, I've reduced location details to more broad continent groups(2). Species data of six continents together with a world summary(3) is shown in the below radial graph. Yearly new discoveries are on the outer ring, and accumulated discoveries (i.e. total species known as of any given year) on the inner circle.

Asia and Africa have the most spiders in the long run. Europe ranks last, with its number of species only one third that of Asia. However, during the 18th and 19th centuries Europe rivaled Asia in the number of most yearly discoveries, probably due to rich naturalist activities and (then) more advanced record-keeping.

Moving from continent view to spider close-ups, I wanted a way to highlight interesting spiders in the context of quantitative data. There were several levels of spider grouping(4) to consider:

  • Several spider species can belong to a genus; e.g. the infamous black widow and its less venomous brown relative belong to the genus Latrodectus.
  • Several genera belong to a family; e.g. Latrodectus and Parasteatoda (genus of the common house spiders) both belong to the family Theridiidae.
  • Several families belong to an infraorder; e.g. Theridiidae and Desidae (family to which belong marine spiders that live inbetween ocean tides) belong to the infraorder Araneomorphae.
  • Several infraorders belong to a suborder; and all suborders, in turn, belong to the umbrella order Araneae — air-breathing arthropods that have eight legs, fangs generally able to inject venom, and spinnerets that extrude silk — known as spiders.

(The order Araneae itself belongs to the class Arachnid, which includes spiders, scorpions, ticks, mites, vinegaroons, and others.)

Since each species was one data point, and very few species are popular enough to have a catchy name like "black widow" — their scientific name often long and not that easy on the eye, I decided to focus on the genus level. For example, St. Helena island, almost half way between South America and Africa, was grouped into Africa. Species with Mediterranean tags were grouped into Europe, even though they very well could be found in some North African port cities. Some remote locations, like the South Georgia and South Sandwich Islands, although closer to South America, were put together with the French Southern and Antarctic Lands into the continent of Antartica, which, in the end, was not shown in the graphs as it has too few species. I think manually editing more than 47 thousand data points, plus a weak knowledge in geography, led to some comatose decisions I'd really like not to repeat in the next project.
