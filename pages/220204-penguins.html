What makes a manly penguin?

In humans, what makes a man noticeably different from the opposite sex tends to make him more appealing. Physical features like the broad shoulders, square jaws, and superior height appear often as qualifiers of (stereotypical) manliness.

I wanted to see if there are equivalents for penguins ‚Äî visible features whose size signals strong male-ness. The below visualization was made from a dataset1 of body measurements from 3332 brush-tailed (also called rump-legged, or Pygoscelis) penguins, including the Ad√©lie, Chinstrap, and Gentoo species.

Choose species

Weight shows the widest gender gap for all three species. This makes sense, as a study of one Ad√©lie colony found that 17.3% of chick losses were from parent deserting the nest due to starvation. The loss could have been avoided if the parent could hold out for a little longer, since the returning mates arrived only a few days later. In the waiting game, it's advantageous to be a large, fat penguin.3 For mommy penguins, fatter husbands mean higher chance of survival for her babies.

Second, while males are generally larger than females, the distinction can be subtle. Some features show more pronounced difference, where we can almost draw a line to separate observations of each gender, like weight for Ad√©lie and Gentoo, beak depth for Gentoo, and to lesser extent beak length for Chinstrap. There are otherwise large areas where blue and green dots migle, where both genders occupy the same range. This also makes sense: in humans, for example, broad shoulders or angular face shapes are not exlusive to men. A combination of several features together, rather than just one, may be more accurate predictor of sex.

Limitions: The highlighted averages in this graph don't reflect conclusions from the research paper1 that accompanies the dataset. It suggests the strongest predictive features of male are:

  • Weight and beak length for Ad√©lie
  • Weight and beak depth for Gentoo
  • Beak length and depth for Chinstrap

With weight the least predictive feature for Chinstrap, and wing length a weak predictor for all three species.

Beside showing that wing length has the smallest gender gap in all three species, the graph fails to reflect most of the rest. This may be because comparing averages is a weak way to determine which features are predictive of sex, as it doesn't take into account overlaps in the observation ranges of two genders, of which there are many, as mentioned earlier. A large overlap means the feature cannot be used to easily distinguish categories.
